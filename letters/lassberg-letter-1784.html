Eppishausen, June 16, 1834. Highly Revered Professor! I am glad that you have received the last shipment so indulgently, and now I send subsequently: 1. Life of Saint Ulrich. 2. The Mirror or Lament of Mary. 3. Love of Amor - unfortunately, 1-2 pages are missing. 4. Master Alebrand's /: Frederick II's horse doctor's:/ on Equine Medicine, due to the appended hymn to Mary, which might likely be by Frauenlob, from which time nearly also the writing might be, but unfortunately incomplete. 5. A spiritual remedy, and 6. Spiritual carnival pastries. The originals of 5 and 6 are in Eppishausen. Believe me, esteemed sir, I am completely convinced that in any case, you will be able to select better what is suitable for your book and its purpose than anyone else; the mere wish to spare you trouble could prompt me to transcribe them myself; I gladly entrust you not only with these but all the books and manuscripts I possess. I must confess that I no longer examined the copy of the Frauendienst upon its return and sought the drawing of the crown in the packaging paper; now, having been made aware by your letter, I found it at the indicated place and repeat my thanks for it. I shall say nothing more about your unfortunate student; such misfortune cuts too deeply into the heart! But what you tell me about Mr. C. Orelly hurt me no less. Such an exquisite scholar should not let himself be hired by Liechtstalers, much less should he act with anger and hatred against the oppressed if he has already submitted to such humiliation; he should not forget that the pitiable Tigurine will never become what Basel already was 300 years ago. I regret having to set aside the good opinion nourished of this man for so many years; but the matter is too obvious to go unnoticed. For my journey, I had a travel companion as far as Kassel, who abruptly canceled on me; I now intend to travel to Cologne by water and from there to Westphalia, and then to the Weser and Leine; but it does not commence before the end of this month. Where and what should I deliver for you in GÃ¶ttingen? Give me many tasks, they shall be executed most punctually: can I bring you anything? Hopefully the already printed but still enclosed Frydank by Wilhelm Grimm. So, I do not take leave yet and await further news from you. Professor Schwab and Ober Tribunal Procurator Abel from Stuttgart have spent a few days with me recently. The latter intends to publish the images from the Parisian codex of the Minnesingers as a facsimile and has shown me some samples, which are not bad at all; the love for the Hohenstaufen and their time has almost become a fixed idea for him, he invests a large part of his considerable fortune in the collection and preservation of German antiquities. Uhland is now working uninterruptedly on his book about the German folk song; but apparently has a couple of other things on the anvil as well, I hope to speak with him on my passage. Have I told you that I have received everything from the 4th volume of Hagen's Minnesingers up to folio 56, Herr Nithart is the last, I think that will now be about half of the whole. I must confess that these so-called Lives of the Poets have exceeded my expectations: sometimes H. von der Hagen certainly makes it easy for himself, for example, he has included the entire Frauendienst according to Tieck's solution, from page 321 to 404. But you can also see quite exactly where H. von der Hagen has left the work to others. Should you wish to use the book during my absence; I will gladly send it to you with much pleasure. In the meantime, may God protect you! From your devoted JvLaÃŸberg.
