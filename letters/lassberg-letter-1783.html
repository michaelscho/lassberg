Translation

Basel, June 13, 1834. Highly esteemed Baron, With embarrassment (for I do not know how I deserve such great kindness), I received your valued letter along with the beautiful enclosures about a week ago. What pleases me most is that you no longer speak of hand gout and that the poems are written as firmly and elegantly as ever. May God keep you in that state! You let a whole sky full of stars rise over the index of my reading book. The previously unpublished items are to be marked with stars. I must express particular thanks for the detailed notes that accompany the individual excerpts: all this shall be faithfully recounted in the preface. Besides all that I have already received, your great kindness lets me hope for two more gifts: for one, you put the request in my mouth, for the other, you have already promised: the holy Ulrich and Amor the Love. Handle them as you did earlier with Ulrich of L. and with the Moor: trust the mail and me, let me choose and copy myself, and your trust shall not be abused: just a small passage from each, no more and nothing else. May I rejoice in advance? My heartfelt thanks for the sympathy you show my unfortunate friend. Unfortunately, whatever the doctor may say optimistically, there is little hope left since the earlier frenzy has turned into madness. I cannot overcome the grief: I miss him in the college, and his visits are missing at home too. A private lecture on Walther vdW in which I had particularly counted on him is now a saddening affair. Good God! Orelli has been here for a week and a half as a landscape tax expert for the library; he accepted the position so it wouldn't fall to a fool or a malevolent person. He values the manuscripts and old prints as if he wanted to offer them for sale to an English lord. And yet, the instruction sounds like an estimate that any possible auction should bring in three times as much. You are traveling to G√∂ttingen. I hope you will give me the opportunity to write to you once more before then, so I can wish you a good journey and ask you to convey my warmest greetings and recommendations. If the printing of the reading book (just now the fourth sheet has been corrected) did not bind me to Basel this summer, your journey would almost distress me, since then an excursion to Lake Constance might have led me past your forest hermitage as well. Well, I hope to see the Codex gemmens argentens once more. The illustration of the crown must have come along: it is not here; I believe it lies between the first pages of the Frauendienst. I did not note the few batzen the artist received for it, because I do not wish to trouble you with such small change; also, the mail would be embarrassed by their percentage. Therefore, I will best retrieve them myself at some point. Farewell. May God keep you in health and let it rain now still: then you will have all the more beautiful travel weather. Remain inclined. Yours sincerely, Wilh. Wackernagel.
